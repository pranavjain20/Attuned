# Session 1 — Mar 17, 2026

Spent this session building the engagement scoring system — computing a 0.0-1.0 score per song from behavioral signals so the matching engine knows which songs I actually love vs ones that just happened to autoplay.

Started with 5 signals: log-normalized play count, completion rate (how much of the song I listen to on average), active play rate (did I intentionally click it), skip rate, and recency. Got the basic implementation working with 19 tests, then ran the first staff tester + auditor cycle. Tester added 38 more tests and found a timezone conversion bug. Auditor flagged 6 issues. Fixed everything, 177 tests passing.

Ran on real data and immediately hit a big problem — 435 duplicate song groups. Spotify re-releases tracks with new URIs, so Namo Namo had 14 plays on one URI and 82 on another instead of 96 combined. Built a dedup module that finds exact (name, artist) matches, picks the best URI, reassigns all listening history, and merges metadata. 52 tests for dedup.

Then the real tuning started. Ride It and Excuses were showing up in the top 10 despite not listening to them in over a year — the recency signal was just checking the last-played date, so one stray play inflated it. Replaced it with recent_play_ratio (plays in last year / total plays). Ride It dropped from 0.83 to 0.47.

Discovered that my two Alexas (bedroom Echo Dot with 2,300+ plays, living room one showing as "not_applicable" with 2,100+ plays) were being penalized — the formula only counted app taps as intentional, but Alexa voice commands show up as "remote" and "playbtn." Shiv Kailash went from active=0.33 to 0.48 after fixing this.

Also found that 3 plays was too few for confident scoring — Paan Ki Dukaan ranked #20 with just 3 perfect plays. Raised the engagement threshold to 5+ plays. Set a separate classification threshold at 2+ plays (1,360 songs) since the LLM just needs to know the musical properties, not behavioral confidence.

Did a side-by-side comparison of recency weight at 0.20 vs 0.15 — went with 0.20 because 0.15 let Ride It and Excuses creep back into the top 25. Final weights: log_play 0.30, completion 0.25, recent_ratio 0.20, active 0.15, (1-skip) 0.10. 669 songs scored. Top 5: Bernie's Chalisa, Namo Namo, Boyfriend, Aayi Nai, Shiv Kailash. 232 tests across 10 files, two full rounds of staff tester + auditor.

# Session 2 — Mar 17, 2026

This session was about tearing down an overengineered state classifier and rebuilding it from first principles.

The previous session's classifier used a weighted scoring system for accumulated fatigue — decomposing WHOOP's recovery score into independent signals (HRV declining 3+ days, RHR rising, sleep debt above 1 SD, red/yellow recovery, both sleep stages deficient), assigning weights (1-2 points each), and triggering fatigue at a threshold of 4/7. The problem: these signals aren't independent. Sleep affects RHR affects HRV affects recovery. The algorithm was second-guessing WHOOP's own computation instead of trusting it.

The fix came from a simple insight: red recovery is never wrong. In 2.5 years of WHOOP data, a red day has never felt good. So instead of decomposing and recombining, the new classifier trusts recovery as the primary signal and uses individual metrics to determine *how* to help, not *whether* it's bad. Recovery tiers: <40 definitively bad, <60 struggling, <80 functional, ≥80 great. Accumulated fatigue checks if recovery is below 60 AND 3+ of the last 5 days were also below 60 — a simple lookback instead of a weighted scoring formula. Sleep deficits are checked at any recovery level. Peak readiness requires ≥80 plus HRV at/above baseline plus no deficits.

Ran the classifier on all 823 days of real WHOOP data. Distribution looked right: 51% baseline, 20% fatigue, 12% poor recovery, 6% peak, 4% each for physical/emotional deficit, 1% poor sleep. Pulled the last 30 days and walked through each one — three fatigue streaks (Feb 19-23, Feb 26-Mar 5, Mar 7-14) with brief 1-2 day recoveries between them. Every classification matched how those days actually felt.

The staff auditor found a real bug: `_is_debt_low` was using `mean - 1*SD` as the threshold, meaning only days with *statistically unusually low* sleep debt could qualify for peak readiness. With real debt numbers averaging 20-25 hours, almost nothing passed. Changed it to just `mean` — debt at or below your personal average. Peak readiness went from 25 to 47 days across the full dataset.

Also renamed `single_bad_night` to `poor_recovery` after talking through what the state actually means emotionally. "Single bad night" sounds like a gentle excuse. What it actually means is: I woke up wrecked, I have to work today, just get me functional. That's a fundamentally different playlist strategy than accumulated fatigue — fatigue playlists ease you into sustained recovery, poor recovery playlists need to bring you UP to functional. Saved this insight for the matching engine build.

354 tests passing. 7 atomic commits. Staff tester found 6 HIGH and 9 MEDIUM coverage gaps, all fixed. Staff auditor found 1 MUST FIX and 5 SHOULD FIX, all resolved.

## Reflection

Something important happened in this session that's worth remembering.

The original classifier was a piece of engineering I was proud of — weighted scoring with 6 independent signals, configurable thresholds, confidence levels based on score magnitude. It felt sophisticated. And that was exactly the problem. It was sophisticated where it should have been simple.

The metrics it was decomposing (HRV, RHR, sleep debt, recovery score) are not independent signals. They're the same signal measured different ways. Sleep quality drives RHR drives HRV drives recovery score. By treating them as independent inputs to a weighted formula, the classifier was essentially trying to outsmart WHOOP's algorithm — taking WHOOP's output, breaking it apart, and reassembling it with our own weights. That's not intelligence. That's noise.

The breakthrough came from stepping back and asking: what are we actually trying to do? We're trying to pick a playlist strategy. Not audit WHOOP's math. Not prove we can build a fancier algorithm. Just: given how the body is doing today, what kind of music helps?

Once the question was right, the answer was obvious. Trust recovery as the primary signal. Use individual metrics only to determine the *type* of help (physical recovery vs emotional support vs just getting through the day). A simple 5-day lookback for fatigue detection. No weighted scores. No decomposition.

This is a pattern I've noticed when working with Claude. There's a natural tendency toward engineering sophistication — more signals, more weights, more configurability. It feels like progress because complexity feels like thoroughness. But complexity has a cost: it's harder to reason about, harder to validate, and harder to trust. When I ran the weighted scorer on real data and couldn't explain why a 29% recovery day was classified as baseline, that wasn't a tuning problem. It was an architecture problem. The system was too clever for its own good.

The lesson applies beyond this project. When building a product, the question isn't "how smart can we make this?" It's "what does this need to do?" The classifier needed to be a translator — take WHOOP's assessment and respond to it. Not an auditor that questions WHOOP's math. Not a research paper that proves we understand HRV science. A translator. And translators work best when they're simple, direct, and honest about what they know.

Going forward, I want to catch this pattern earlier. Before building anything complex, ask: am I solving the actual problem, or am I engineering for the sake of engineering? If the answer to "why is this complicated?" is "because the problem is complicated," keep going. If the answer is "because I wanted to be thorough," stop and simplify.
